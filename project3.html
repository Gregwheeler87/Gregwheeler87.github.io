Case Study

MealEasy

Have you considered the amount of time and effort it takes to figure out what’s for dinner and then actually make it happen? MealEasy wants to make it easier than ever before to plan your meals & shop in a quick trip so families can spend more time on things that matter.

The Problem

Mom & Dad already have enough on their mind with work, making sure the house is kept up, kid’s soccer games, asking 5 million times if homework is done, paying the bills and this is just the tip of the iceberg. The last thing they want to take time to consider after a full day is “what’s for dinner?”.

The Solution

MealEasy eliminates “what’s for dinner” so families can focus on what matters. It’s a clean and easy-to-use app to plan meals and shop in one quick trip for the week. Gone are the last minute trips to the store and the ever-nagging question “where’s that recipe”? Let’s dive into story of MealEasy.

View Prototype

The Process

Survey

After surveying busy families from all walks of life, there was one thing in common. It was the amount of time spent on the process of planning meals for the week.

Below are my key discoveries:
  • Most people cook 3-4 times a week
  • 70% of people use paper to create their grocery list. Of the 70%, 87.5% said they would use an app if it was as easy as using paper
  • Users want the grocery list categories to closely match the store layout
  • Nearly 90% of the users say they would rather cook a recipe they already like than try a new one
  • 2 of the most important factors when choosing what to eat are cook/prep time and amount of food it will make
View Survey

All of these discoveries lead to the amount of time it takes to accomplish these tasks. So the goal with this app was simple: reduce the amount of time.

Competitive Analysis

With the goal being saving time, I performed a SWOT analysis on 3 competitors to see if they were solving this problem.

Mealime
  • Strengths
    • Onboarding process- gets specific with asking about allergies, dislikes, menu type, etc
    • Overall clean & easy to understand UI
  • Weaknesses
    • No dates to see the plan for the week
    • Importing recipe feature is hard to find
  • Opportunities
    • Include bar-code scanner and add an option to include the price of items in the grocery list
    • Add in more recipes for different types of meals (breakfast, lunch, etc)
  • Threats
    • One time purchase competitors with same features
    • Features can be copied
Plan To Eat
  • Strengths
    • 30-day free trial
    • Importing recipe from web seems to be pretty accurate & easy
  • Weaknesses
    • No pre-loaded recipes
    • Confusing and sometimes busy UI/navigation
    • Doesn’t show what items in the shopping list are related to what recipe
  • Opportunities
    • Onboarding process- explain how to use app
    • Tailor messaging to target audience
  • Threats
    • One time purchase competitors with same features
    • Change in customer needs
MealBoard
  • Strengths
    • Pantry feature where you can save what you already have in stock
    • Bar-code scanner
  • Weaknesses
    • No free version, $2.99 in app store
    • Nutritional info is not easily accessible-have to go outside of the app
  • Opportunities
    • Better UI with instructions for recipes
    • Better onboarding process
  • Threats
    • Features can be copied
    • Apps with a free trial that have similar features

There were a few opportunites found.

First was the ability to easily import a recipe vs providing recipes. This was a big one because as stated in the survey nearly 90% of people wanted to cook a recipe they already were familiar with not a new one.

Second, because of the nature of meal planning apps, they were crawling with features which led to frustration due to the amount of time it takes to learn the app. This was an opportunity MealEasy could help with.

Sketches

After creating the user flows, it was time to get these ideas down visually.

Doing the sketches first provided a birdseye view which helped with the wireframes.

Low-fidelity Wireframes

I started with lo-fi wireframes to get a feel for where the content would go and how that affected the user flows I created previously. Then it was on to the hi-fi wireframes with the copy I created in the content strategy. This helped me to really see how the layout from the lo-fi wireframes supported the copy. A few iterations that came about this journey.

    On the intro screens, I realized I didn’t show the progress or how long the intro screens would take or give them the option to skip it. To solve that I included 3 dots with one circled in representing which screen you were on. I also included a skip button in the top right corner.

    The “meet app screen” with the 3 steps was seen as confusing because the 3 steps were housed in button style rectangles. To resolve this I removed the rectangles and added small circles around the numbers.

View Wireframes

High-Fidelity Mockups

The app itself has a quite a few things going on. You have 3 big features of adding recipes, a grocery list and a meal planner all in one app. So naturally the scope of this app could easily have gone out of control. What helped was continuing to ask the questions, does this decision make it more simple, easy or convenient? If not, it needed to go. During the mockups phase a few things came up.

    The meal planner screen was minimalstic but it didn’t communicate “wow” visually. This was important as it was the first screen the user interacted after going through the intro screens. To solve that, I created a screen with small cards. It still was very simple but was more visually appealing.

    On the recipe screen I included a CTA at the bottom of the screen. It was easily identifyable with no recipes loaded but as more recipes were added, the question soon became would this button be seen anymore? To solve that I added a plus sign in the top right corner.

User Testing

The 3 main tasks during the tests where to: add a new recipe, schedule a meal on the meal plan screen, check off an item on the grocery list and remove the loaded meal from the meal plan. Below are my findings:

Onboarding screens

The progress dots on the bottom made users think the screens were swipeable. The solution here was to allow user to swipe in addition to using the button.

Meal plan card style

Once the meal was loaded in the meal plan screen, the user thought they lost the date. The placement of the date at the bottom made it harder to identify. The solution here was to swap the title of the recipe and the date.

The pencil icon was too small

All users used the edit pencil to remove. The process was very smooth. No friction here. The only thing I noticed was one of the users asked if it was a pencil making me think the pencil was a little too small. The solution was to increase the size of the pencil icon.

Branding & Identity

It started with list-making with the words simple and meal-planning. This led to identifying 3 words that would be a filter for all design decisions. The 3 words were: Simple, Easy & Convenient. Everything from color, typography, button design to copy needed to go through these 3 words.

Conceptual Ideation

Logo Design

The sketches of the logo were both from an abstract to a more literal feel. Keeping the words simple, easy & convenient in mind, it made sense to go with a minimalistic logo.

To keep it simple with typography I went with one font: open sans. The font is a modern sans serif font with quite a few weight options to help communicate throughout the app.

The colors needed to support the minimalstic approach. In addition, the accent color purple helps the food/grocery app stand out as most of the food apps are red, orange or green.

Conclusion

The overall challenge here was designing a beefy (pun intended) meal planning app that was also simple, easy and convenient to use.

Idenfiying the pain points and ultimately realizing that the time spent on planning, shopping and cooking was the one thing that needed to be reduced provided clarity along the journey.

Throughout the process it was important for me to continue to ask does this solve the problem? Does it support the overall goal of saving time? After many iterations and testing I believe this is a great start to a meal planning app that busy families will love.
